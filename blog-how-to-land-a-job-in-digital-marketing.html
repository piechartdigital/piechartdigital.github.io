How to land a job in the digital marketing industry

A practical guide to getting started, building useful experience and finding your route into a digital marketing career.

By Damien Connell
← Back to blogs

1st July 2026 • Digital Marketing • Careers

Digital marketing is one of those industries that can feel exciting and confusing at the same time. There are loads of different routes in, loads of job titles, and loads of skills people tell you that you need to learn.

The good news is that you do not need to know everything before you start. Nobody does. Most people working in digital marketing built their career by learning a bit, testing things, getting experience, making mistakes, improving, and gradually finding the area that suited them best.

If you are trying to land your first job in digital marketing, this guide will help you understand the main routes in, what skills are useful, and how to make yourself more employable when you are starting out.

First, understand what digital marketing actually includes

Digital marketing is not one single job. It covers a lot of different areas, which is why it can feel overwhelming at first.

Some people work mainly on paid ads. Some focus on social media. Some are copywriters, SEO specialists, email marketers, content creators, website editors, data analysts or campaign managers. Some roles are very creative, some are very technical, and some are a mix of both.

Common areas within digital marketing include:

  • Social media marketing
  • Paid advertising, including Meta ads and Google PPC
  • SEO and organic search
  • Content marketing
  • Email marketing
  • Copywriting
  • Website updates and landing pages
  • Analytics, reporting and conversion tracking
  • Creative strategy and campaign planning

You do not need to master all of these to get started. In fact, it is usually better to understand the basics of a few areas, then build deeper knowledge in one or two.

You do not always need a marketing degree

A marketing degree can help, but it is not the only route into the industry. Plenty of people land digital marketing jobs through apprenticeships, internships, self-learning, volunteering, freelancing, side projects or moving across from another role.

Employers often care more about whether you can think clearly, communicate well, understand the basics, show initiative and prove that you have actually tried to learn the job.

If you have a degree, great. Use it. If you do not, do not assume the door is closed. Digital marketing is a practical industry, and practical evidence matters.

Learn the basics before applying everywhere

Before applying for jobs, spend some time learning the fundamentals. You do not need to become an expert, but you should understand the language of the industry.

Useful things to learn include:

  • What SEO is and why it matters
  • How paid ads work at a basic level
  • What makes a good social media post
  • How businesses use landing pages to convert traffic
  • What a call to action is
  • How email marketing is used
  • What impressions, clicks, conversions and conversion rate mean
  • How to read basic website or campaign performance

You do not need to pay for expensive courses at the start. There are plenty of free resources from Google, Meta, HubSpot, YouTube creators, blogs and digital marketing newsletters. The important thing is to learn actively rather than just passively watching videos.

Build something you can show

One of the best ways to stand out when you have little experience is to create proof that you can do the work.

This does not have to be a huge project. It could be a small website, a social media content plan, a mock campaign, a blog, a newsletter, a simple SEO audit or a few sample ad concepts.

For example, you could:

  • Create a basic marketing plan for a local business
  • Write three sample blog posts around a topic you understand
  • Design a simple Instagram content calendar
  • Build a one-page website or landing page
  • Run a small personal project and track what you learn
  • Audit a brand’s website and suggest improvements

This gives you something to talk about in interviews. Instead of just saying “I am interested in marketing”, you can say “I created this, here is what I was trying to achieve, here is what I learned, and here is what I would improve next time.”

Try to get real experience, even if it is small

Real experience is valuable, but it does not always have to come from a full-time job. If you are starting out, look for small opportunities to help people.

You might offer to help a local club with social posts, support a family business with Google Business Profile updates, write content for a small charity, or help a friend improve their website copy.

The aim is not to work for free forever. The aim is to get a few real examples under your belt so you understand how marketing works outside of theory.

Even small projects teach you useful lessons about communication, deadlines, feedback, business goals and what clients actually care about.

Understand that marketing is about business goals

A common mistake when starting out is thinking digital marketing is only about making things look good or getting likes.

Good marketing is connected to business goals. A business might want more enquiries, more sales, more bookings, better quality leads, more awareness, stronger trust, more repeat customers or a better online reputation.

When you apply for jobs, try to show that you understand this. Instead of only saying you like social media, talk about how social media can build trust, support campaigns, communicate offers and move people closer to taking action.

Pick an area to focus on, but stay curious

When you are starting out, it can help to choose one main area to focus on. This makes your learning feel more manageable and gives you a clearer direction.

For example, you might decide:

  • You want to become a social media executive
  • You want to work in paid ads
  • You want to focus on SEO and content
  • You want to become a copywriter
  • You want to work on websites and landing pages

That does not mean you ignore everything else. The best marketers usually understand how different areas connect. Paid ads need landing pages. Social content needs clear messaging. SEO needs good content. Websites need strong calls to action.

Pick a starting point, but keep learning around it.

Make your CV specific, not generic

A lot of entry-level marketing CVs say similar things: hard-working, creative, passionate, good communicator. Those things are fine, but they do not prove much on their own.

Try to make your CV more specific. Mention tools you have used, projects you have worked on, campaigns you have studied, content you have created or results you have helped with.

Even if the experience is small, make it practical:

  • Created a 30-day content plan for a local business
  • Wrote and uploaded blog content using basic SEO principles
  • Used Canva to create branded social media graphics
  • Reviewed Google Analytics data to understand website traffic
  • Helped improve website copy and calls to action

That sounds much stronger than simply saying you are interested in digital marketing.

Use LinkedIn properly

LinkedIn can be useful when you are trying to get into digital marketing, but only if you use it sensibly.

You do not need to become a motivational influencer. Just make sure your profile is clear, up to date and aligned with the kind of role you want.

A good beginner LinkedIn profile should include:

  • A clear headline, such as “Aspiring Digital Marketing Executive”
  • A short summary explaining what you are learning and what role you want
  • Any projects, volunteering or relevant experience
  • Skills such as social media, copywriting, SEO basics, paid ads basics or analytics
  • A link to any portfolio, blog or project work

You can also follow agencies, marketing managers, recruiters and brands you admire. Comment thoughtfully where relevant. Share small things you are learning. Keep it simple and professional.

Do not ignore apprenticeships and junior roles

A lot of people aim straight for “Digital Marketing Executive” roles, but there are other routes in.

Apprenticeships can be a strong option, especially if you want structured learning alongside practical work. Junior marketing assistant roles, social media assistant roles, content assistant roles and admin roles within marketing teams can also be good starting points.

Your first job does not need to be your dream job. It needs to get you into the environment, help you learn, and give you experience you can build on.

Agency, in-house or freelance?

There are different types of marketing careers, and each one teaches you different things.

In an agency, you might work across several clients. This can be fast-paced and a great way to learn different industries quickly.

In-house means working for one business or brand. You may get deeper knowledge of that company and have more time to understand its audience, products and goals.

Freelancing gives you independence, but it can be harder when you are brand new because you also need to find clients, manage expectations and handle the business side.

There is no perfect route. If you are starting out, agency or in-house experience can give you useful structure before you think about freelancing.

What employers look for in entry-level candidates

You are not expected to know everything at entry level. But employers do look for signs that you are serious, organised and willing to learn.

Helpful qualities include:

  • Good writing and communication skills
  • Curiosity about how businesses grow online
  • Basic understanding of social media and digital channels
  • Willingness to learn tools and platforms
  • Attention to detail
  • Ability to take feedback without taking it personally
  • Evidence that you have tried to create or improve something yourself

Enthusiasm helps, but practical effort matters more. Show that you have already taken steps to learn the industry.

Prepare for interviews with examples

In interviews, you may be asked why you want to work in digital marketing, what campaigns you like, what brands you follow, or how you would improve a piece of content.

Prepare examples before the interview. Choose a brand you think does social media well. Find a website with strong calls to action. Look at a paid ad and think about what makes it work. Read a few recent marketing articles so you can talk about the industry with confidence.

If you have your own small project, talk about it honestly. Explain what you tried, what worked, what did not, and what you would do next.

Keep learning, but do not get stuck only learning

Courses are useful, but it is easy to get stuck in “learning mode” and never actually do anything.

Digital marketing is practical. You learn more when you apply the ideas. Write the post. Build the page. Create the mock campaign. Review the data. Ask for feedback. Improve it.

A completed imperfect project is usually more useful than ten unfinished courses.

Final thoughts on starting a digital marketing career

Landing a job in digital marketing is not about knowing every platform, every acronym or every tactic on day one.

It is about showing that you understand the basics, care about how businesses communicate online, and are willing to learn by doing.

Start small. Learn the foundations. Build examples. Get some real experience. Make your CV specific. Use LinkedIn sensibly. Apply for junior roles, apprenticeships and assistant positions. Then keep going.

Digital marketing is a broad industry, which is exactly why there are so many ways in. Once you get started, you can find the route that fits your strengths, whether that is paid ads, social media, content, SEO, websites, strategy or something else entirely.
